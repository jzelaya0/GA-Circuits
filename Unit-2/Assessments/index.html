Introduction

Before we begin, let’s remind ourselves of the learning objectives for this activity:

  • Differentiate between types of assessment and when you should use each
  • Identify 5-6 classroom assessment techniques to add to your lesson plan

Learning objectives outline the goals of the lesson.

Scaffolding provides the structure that enables your students to reach these goals.

Assessments check whether or not students have met these goals.

You’re probably familiar with tests, quizzes, and projects from your time in school.

These are formal, graded assessments announced way in advance.

You may not have, on the other hand, been aware of the subtle, non-graded assessments your instructors were conducting throughout the lesson.

Informal assessments or checks for understanding allow instructors and students to test students’ knowledge of the material during class.

Examples of checks for understanding include

  • having students raise their hands if they agree
  • having students stop and write down their answers before sharing with a partner
  • having students complete short activities in groups.

While assessments can take many different forms and serve many different purposes, we are going to focus on the types of assessments present in your lesson and course.

WHAT: Defining Assessments

An assessment is any tool or method that an instructor uses to gauge if and to what extent students are learning.

Assessments are the “check” against learning objectives. Have your students met all of the learning objectives for the lesson? The results of assessments will measure this for you.

There are two types of assessments you’ll be executing in your class, formative and summative assessments.

Formative assessments measure students’ learning during their learning experiences, as they are ‘forming’ their knowledge.

Formative assessments build toward summative assessments (assessments that measure the “sum” of one’s learning experience).

One type of formative assessment is used often at GA.

We call these “checks for understanding.”

These short, in-class activities are actually assessments in disguise!

They allow instructors to informally observe students, gauge their comprehension, and adjust their lesson or provide support accordingly.

For example: gauging student comprehension of new material by having students “stand if they agree” with specific statements at the end of a lecture.

YOUR ROLE: Assesments

Knowing how and when you’ll be asked to facilitate each type of assessment will help you guide students toward reaching the expressed outcomes of the class.

Identifying the summative assessments, such as homework and projects, provided in your course materials will enable you to keep the end goal of each lesson and the course in mind.

Determining how and when you’ll add or facilitate formative assessments in the form of specific checking for understanding techniques and guiding the activity portions of your class.

Adding checks for understanding to your lesson is one of the most overlooked but critical parts of lesson preparation.

These techniques are determined by the instructor and not included in most lesson plans.

Similarly, it is also important to get really comfortable with managing in-class guided practice, or the “We Do” part of your lesson plan.

New instructors may want to skip these portions of the lesson because they are unsure about how to facilitate student practice in the classroom.

This is one of the most common new teacher errors that we see at GA.

Evaluating Homework & Projects

  • Summative assessments measure the sum of students’ learning experiences for a lesson or a course.
  • As such, they should be evaluated against the learning objectives for a lesson (for homework) or course (for the final project).

For both forms of summative assessment (homework and projects), students want to know:

  • “Did I achieve all of the objectives?”
  • “If so, I want confirmation.”
  • “If not, I’d like guidance and an explanation.”

In fact, wanting more specific feedback about how they’re doing in relation to the learning objectives of the course is one of the most common things requested by students on their GA surveys!

In general, feedback on all assessments should:

  • Be timely
  • Be aligned with objectives
  • Be precise
  • Be Balanced

Be Timely

  • Feedback loses value the longer the turnaround time. Aim to give brief comments quickly rather than delayed ,detailed responses.
  • It’s easier to recommend and implement small changes - for both parties!
  • Pick the highest value thing the student can work on improving.

In general, feedback on all assessments should:

  • Be timely
  • Be aligned with objectives
  • Be precise
  • Be Balanced

Be Aligned With Objectvies

  • Your comments should focus on how well the student achieved the objectives of the lesson, and how much of that is evident in the student’s work.
  • Limit yourself to comments that directly address the achievement (or not) of these skills.

In general, feedback on all assessments should:

  • Be timely
  • Be aligned with objectives
  • Be precise
  • Be Balanced

Be Precise

  • Offer broad and specific commentary on student skills.
  • Highlight a trend or issue and point to one specific example as indicative of the trend/issue throughout the work.
  • It’s very important to give precise praise and critique so that the student understands the feedback without your help.

In general, feedback on all assessments should:

  • Be timely
  • Be aligned with objectives
  • Be precise
  • Be Balanced

Be Balanced

  • Provide at least one “grow” and “glow” for each piece of work.
  • Aim for one piece of positive feedback (something the student did well) for every suggestion for improvement.
  • Students learn just as much from what you affirm as being correct as what you suggest could be different.
