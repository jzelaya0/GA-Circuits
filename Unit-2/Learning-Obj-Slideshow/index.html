So what’s the big deal with learning objectives?

Simply put, understanding the learning objectives of your lesson will help you keep the lesson and your students on track by reminding you and the students of the lesson’s end goal.

Four of the most common new teacher mistakes regarding learning objectives include:

  1. Not utilizing learning objectives to focus the lesson.

  2. Not sharing the learning objectives with students, so that students are also aware of the goals of the lesson.

  3. Not writing the learning objectives in a place where they will be visible to students the entire time.

  4. Not using learning objectives as a literal and figurative checklist for what should be accomplished during the lesson.

WHAT: Defining Learning Objectives

Learning objectives are the expressed goals for a lesson. They tell the student what they will be able to do, and how they will know they got there.

Learning objectives possess 6 distinct traits. The learning objectives for your lesson were designed to be:

  • Attainable within the lesson time
  • Measurable
  • Specific
  • Framed in terms of “students will be able to…” (SWBAT)
  • Clearly fitted with the overall course goals
  • Ordered based on complexity and a learning hierarchy (Depth of Knowledge, Bloom’s Taxonomy)

Being aware of how learning objectives gradually increase in difficulty and build off of each other will help you understand the lesson.

You’ll notice that your lesson starts with simpler objectives and gradually increases to more complex ones.

The level of complexity of learning objectives are determined by the verbs used.

To illustrate this point, look at this Depth of Knowledge Chart.

The chart categorizes verbs based on the depth of knowledge their execution accomplishes.

It reads clockwise from top, Levels 1-4.

It’s important to note that the higher levels require more time to learn and perform.

For example, Level 4 “synthesis” skills can be demonstrated through complex tasks that are done over a longer period of time - i.e. student projects done at the end of the course.

Remember, lesson objectives will never get to Level 4 within one lesson. Level 4 requires long-term knowledge which is only made possible over time, with repetition, and by solidifying the lower 3 levels of knowledge.

YOUR ROLE: Learning Objectives & Lesson Delivery

Remember, lesson delivery bridges the gap between the materials you are given and the experience you create in the classroom.

Identifying lesson delivery tactics allows you to make the lesson your own and bring the lesson to life while also ensuring that students reach common course goals.

There are 2 major ways you’ll be utilizing learning objectives in your lesson delivery.

1. Internalize your objectives before the lesson to focus your instruction on getting students to these specific outcomes.

2. Make learning objectives and palpable to students during class to share responsibility for reaching these goals.

1. Internalize your objectives before the lesson to focus your instruction on getting students to these specific outcomes.

Remember, you cannot change the learning objectives of your lesson.

It’s better to focus on what kinds of elements you can add to enhance students’ understanding of these objectives when delivering the lesson.

Elements you can add include

  • a “hook” - way to engage students and start the lesson
  • illustrations and examples that will bring the content to life
  • anecdotes from your experience in the industry
  • checks for understanding
  • specific, scaffolded questions to ask students
  • talking points around particular topics
  • additional student activities

2. Make learning objectives and palpable to students during class to share responsibility for reaching these goals.

Beginning of lesson:

  • Write learning objectives on the board and leave up for duration of class
  • Announce to class

Throughout lesson:

  • Reference objectives on the board
  • Check off objectives as you accomplish them
  • Acknowledge objectives when transitioning from one skill to another or when reminding students of the goals of the lesson

End of lesson:

  • Review as a part of your lesson closing
  • Check for students’ understanding
  • Preview how the skills factor into the homework or project assignment
