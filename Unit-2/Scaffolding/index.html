Introduction

Before we begin, let’s remind ourselves of the learning objective for this activity:

  • Identify how scaffolding impacts your lessons

Remember, learning objectives outline the goals of the lesson.

Scaffolding provides the structure that enables your students to reach those goals.

Scaffolding a lesson ensures that the first step in learning a new task is easy.

This motivates students to continue.

Each step afterward builds on the skill before it, increasing in complexity and difficulty, until the student is able to perform the complex skill by themselves.

There are many different applications of scaffolding in education.

We’ll be focusing on one specific model, “I DO, WE DO, YOU DO” in order to highlight how a lesson gradually moves from instructor-led to student-led activities.

WHAT: Defining Scaffolding

To scaffold a lesson plan means to break down a complex task, skill, or concept into simpler, incremental steps or parts where the first step is the easiest.

The model we’ll be using to explore this concept is the “I Do - We Do - You Do” lesson plan model.

“I Do” - “We Do” - “You Do” lesson model

There are many different ways to create a lesson.

One of the simplest ways is using an “I Do” - “We do” - “You do” model.

“I Do” - “We Do” - “You Do” lesson model

This model exemplifies scaffolded lesson planning because of its gradual transfer from instructor-led to student-led activities.

Let’s explore the idea behind the I Do- We Do- You- Do model.

“I Do”

Introduction to new material & modeling.

Instructor introduces the skill and shows how to “do” it.

“I Do”

Lecturing & theory are kept short.

Instructor does most of the “heavy lifting” but keeps students engaged through opportunities to participate, asking questions, and various other engagement techniques and checks for understanding.

“We Do”

Guided practice. Students and instructors practice the skill together.

Students have a chance to experiment and work together while instructor facilitates and can support, answer questions, or correct students on the spot.

“We Do”

Note: Exercises like code-alongs or walk-throughs of tools (i.e. Photoshop), where the instructor is demo-ing and the students are silently following along is not a “We Do.”

For a true “We Do,” the students must be leading the activity and doing most of the intellectual “heavy lifting”.

“You Do”

Independent practice.

Students have the opportunity to assess their own knowledge by working on their own.

This may come in the form of homework in some cases.

Gradual Release

The idea that knowledge, questioning, and application builds over time.

This model encourages the gradual transfer of learning responsibility from the instructor to the student.

Framing Scaffolding

The motto that “the first step is easy” also applies to the elements that frame a lesson -- namely, the opening (or “hook”) and debrief and closing.

  • Opening
  • Closing

Opening Framing

What is about to happen, why, and how?

  • The opening, or “hook” frames and builds up to the introduction of the learning objectives.
  • Typically NOT included in the lesson plan or presentation slides.

You’ll aim to answer these questions in your opening:

  • How will you communicate what is about to happen, how it will happen, and its importance?
  • How will you communicate connections to previous lessons?
  • How will you engage students and capture their interest?

Closing Framing

Wrap up, recap the lesson, and transition to students’ next steps.

  • The closing debriefs lesson activities, summarizes key takeaways, recaps learning objectives, and, in general, synthesizes the learning of the lesson and outlines students’ next steps post-lesson.
  • Typically NOT included in the lesson plan or presentation slides.

Think about how to reaffirm takeaways from the “We Do” or “You Do” activities.

These takeaways may have been recognized in small group or individual settings, but it’s good practice to verbalize these insights and conclusions with the class during this time.

How can you get them from processing the details of the lesson to connecting back to the learning objectives and long-term learning goal?

You’ll aim to answer these questions in your closing:

  • How will students summarize and state the significance of what they learned?
  • How does the lesson relate to the task they’ll be asked to do?
  • How will you preview the homework assignment and any next steps?

Identify your role in each of the scaffolded sections: “I Do” - “We Do” - “You Do.”

I DO

  • Identify talking points, examples from the industry and/or your experience, opportunities to check for understanding.
  • Identify specific questions related to the objectives to ask students.
  • Replace examples that you and/or your class demographic is not familiar with.

I DO

  • Identify one real-world example and/or an example “end product” you could show students to help frame “what success looks like” for the lesson.
  • Identify what the students should be doing during this time. For example, should they be taking notes? Should they be writing down key vocabulary words that they are learning from your slides? Summarizing the ideas their own words?
  • Keep this section time-boxed. Running over on your “I Do” section in an attempt to “cover more material” and skipping the “We Do” part (when the students actually practice) is not good teaching.

WE DO

  • Review the directions for the activity and clarify. Identify how you’ll make directions visible for the duration of the activity.
  • Rehearse giving the directions for the activity beforehand.
  • Determine how you’ll utilize TAs to help facilitate the activity and check students’ group work.

WE DO

  • Familiarize yourself with the time allotted for each activity.
  • Write specific questions that prompt students to reflect on the activity. Check out this one-pager of question stems that align with the Depth of Knowledge chart.
  • Create talking points around the main takeaways from the activity that you’d like students to uncover.

YOU DO

  • Review the directions for the homework and determine how you’ll introduce it to the class.
  • Rehearse the directions for the homework.
  • Determine specific questions to ask students to see if they understand what the homework is asking of them.
  • Identify at least one check for understanding that prompts each student to reflect on what they learned that day.

YOU DO

  • Rehearse the checks for understanding before the lesson.
  • Preview what students will learn next class and link it to the value of completing the homework.
  • Identify any extra resources to help students with the homework or extra reading to pique their interest.

Adhere to timings

Adhere to timings

The most effective instructors are those that time box each part of the lesson and adhere to these timings.

For example, if you are running over on your “I Do,” it is better to move onto your activity (“Do”) rather than covering more material and skipping the opportunity for students to practice the skill.

Don’t worry if you don’t hit your timings exactly; you’ll get better at estimating how long things should take.

The important thing is that you are planning for each part of the lesson and keeping time during the lesson to hold yourself accountable.
