Before we begin, let’s remind ourselves of the learning objectives for this activity:

  1. Describe the main principles of adult learning theory

Knowing how adults learn helps us understand our students and how to create effective, high-impact learning experiences for them.

We utilize a set of six adult learning principles to guide our approach to instruction at GA. These principles structure everything we do, from lesson design, to instructor training, to building better products.

Learning experiences for adults need to include

  1. Opportunites for self direction
  2. Opportunites to draw on life experiences
  3. Relevance to learner
  4. Problem-centered learning objectives
  5. Social elements
  6. Active learning

Principles of Adult Learning Theory

The principles of adult learning theory, or andragogy, prescribe the types of learning activities and environments that maximize adults’ learning potential.

GA instructors should use the following principles as a checklist when planning their lessons to ensure that their lessons align with best practices for teaching adults.

The Basics: Adult Learning Theory

In the 1980s, Malcolm Knowles popularized andragogy (the method and practice of teaching adult learners).

The basic premise is that adults need to be active learners; they need to do in order to learn.

This information in this session is broken down into two components:

  1. What Adults Need (Principles of Adult Learning Theory)

  2. How this Principle Applies to Your Lesson (Lesson Checklist)

1. Opportunities for Self-Direction

Definition: Adults need guidance in the beginning, but they eventually want to direct their own learning and be self-sufficient.

1. Opportunities for Self-Direction

What this means for your lesson:

  • Break tasks down into simpler steps. This will give students a procedure to follow on their own, outside of class.

  • Include both mechanical and strategic thinking steps:

  • Mechanical steps (i.e. how to use Twitter) are easier to learn on one’s own.

  • Strategic thinking steps (i.e. how to determine if a Twitter campaign is right for your start up) tend to be more opaque because they outline how to think about a problem and are often not written down by experts, to whom such knowledge is routinely accessed. (i.e. Twitter gurus don’t need to write down how they determine if a campaign is right for a client).

  • Suggest next steps at the end of the lesson. If students want to learn more or are interested in related topics, what should they do? How can they continue to self-direct their own learning?

2. Opportunities to draw on life experiences.

Definition: Adults need to relate new learnings to past experiences. They are not blank slates. They have tons of prior knowledge that should be recognized and utilized.

2. Opportunities to draw on life experiences.

What this means for your lesson:

  • Involve students to select methods, materials and resources for instruction. Use the beginning of the course or lesson to ask students what they are most interested in learning. During the course or lesson, continue to check in regarding the relevance and pace of instruction. Throughout the course or lesson, ask students to share their own experiences and determine as a group which examples and skills are most critical to learn.

  • Allow for student choice. As often as possible, allow students to select the context, topic, industry, or company on which an assignment or project can be based. This enables students to capitalize on their prior knowledge. The result is that they’re more engaged and can extend prior and new learnings.

  • Use examples that relate to prior knowledge. The advantage you have with adults is that you can relate complex relationships and systems to ones they already know about, facilitating a quicker understanding of new material.
