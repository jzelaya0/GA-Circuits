Growth Mindset Strategy

Students need to know information about their performance so that they may use this information to confirm or improve during their next try.

That being said, how feedback is delivered and framed directly impacts students’ ability to act on and apply the feedback.

This is why GA’s Feedback Method is grounded by the concept of growth mindset

So what is growth mindset?

Let’s explore.

What do you consider one of your weaknesses? What is something you’re “not a natural” at?

For example, some people consider themselves “not a math person” or “unathletic.”

This belief about your intelligence or ability could be considered “fixed” -- meaning that it’s unlikely to change -- but mostly because you think it can’t be changed, no matter how much time you put into it. This contributes to a “fixed mindset.”

Growth mindset is a term coined by Carol Dweck, Ph. D.

CAROL DWEK, PH.D

  • Stanford Professor of Psychology and one of the world's leading researchers in the field of motivation
  • Interested in why people succeed and how to foster success

Growth mindset, he explains, is the belief that intelligence is malleable, not fixed. He outlines how this type of mindset encourages students to seek out challenges instead of quitting when things are hard.

He links growth mindset to success ;individuals with a growth mindset succeed precisely because they turn negative performance feedback into motivation for learning and improving.

Let’s outline some of the key differentiators between fixed and growth mindset:

And now let’s compare examples of Fixed vs. Growth Mindset

FIXED VS GROWTH MINDSET

  • Fixed: effort is for the incapable. Growth: effort is for learning.
  • Fixed: stop at adversity (protect ego). Growth: identity setbacks with growth, try again and learn, enjoy hard problems.
  • Fixed: hide from feedback. Growth seek feedback.
  • Fixed: compare themselves with others. Growth: compare with their previous self.
  • Fixed: "you are smart at this" - focus on fixed ability/result. Growth: "you must have tried really hard" - focus on process and progress.

Inhibits Growth Mindset

  • Beliefs or behaviors that prevent someone from hearing or engaging with the feedback
  • Believing that you are stupid or special
  • Having difficulty separating self-identity from performance

As a new teacher, you might discover that you have a fixed approach to learning new things sometimes, too.

That’s okay! You can decide to work toward a growth mindset.

HOW DO WE DEVELOP THIS MINDSET?

  • Listen for the fixed mindset voice inside
    • "You might look stupid."
    • "Someone else is better than you."
    • "I told you" when you make a mistake.
  • Talk about it
    • "If I don't do this hard thing, I'll never grow."
    • "If I hit an obstable now, I'll try a new strategy next time."

ENCOURAGING GROWTH MINDSET

  • Focus on the process of learning.
  • Celebrate struggle and learnings, not results.
  • Don't water down a challenge for a struggling student.
  • Ask them to take notes
  • Prompt them for clarifying questions.
  • Ask: What is the ideal outcome of this session?

Growth mindset anchors our best practices and approach to giving feedback.

Best Practices

  • Lead with the belief that intelligence is malleable (growth mindset)
  • Focus on learning and improvement
  • Separate behavior from the person. Distinguish between the description of student’s ineffective practice from commentary on who they are as a person
  • Let the student lead. Ask questions to get the student to determine challenges, solutions, and take the lead in directing the conversation
  • Ask the student what’s next. Involve the student in determining his/her next steps
  • Write down next steps and identify a time frame (i.e. next homework, throughout the week) when the student should aim to practice the skill or complete the steps

Common Bad Practices:

  • Telling the student that they are “good at” one type of task like logical thinking vs. poor at another like designing
  • Identifying students as smart or slow, instead of describing their achievement in terms of skills they have learned or have yet to learn
  • Not having a growth mindset about a student’s soft skills. For example, saying that a student is “bad a group work” instead of coaching them at that skill just like you would with a technical skill.
