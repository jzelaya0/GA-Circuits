How do you know the extent to which you’re succeeding as an instructor?

In what ways will you learn about your progress as an instructor and the extent to which you’re leading a successful program experience?

Let’s explore two key concepts for the answer

  • Feedback Loop - Ensuring that we actively iterate based on data
  • Instructor Success Metrics - The data we look at

Feedback Loop

You’ll be working with your instructional team and producer to maximize students’ - as well as your own - success in the course.

There are two primary ways that you’ll receive feedback on your class’s progress and your professional development as a teacher from your producer:

  • Data Review (student sentiment)

    Reviewing student and class data (with producer; as instructional team). Primarily based on student sentiment regarding their course experience (surveys), with a few opportunities to give feedback on concepts (exit tickets).

  • Observation and Feedback (instructional quality)

    Reviewing instructor performance data. Based on observations of your teaching performance against the Instructor Rubric.

Data Review (Student Sentiment)

We touched on data review as a shared responsibility between your instructional team and producer. It’s important to keep in mind that most classroom data discussed between instructors and producers is sentiment-based, meaning that students are rating their feelings regarding their classroom experience.

We take these types of metrics seriously as a user-driven organization, but we also recognize that student feedback needs to be paired with feedback from team members trained on instructional best practices in order to recognize and value your development as an instructor throughout the course.

Let’s take a look at how student sentiment metrics factor into your overall performance evaluation at GA.

Data Review (Student Sentiment)

Regular data review

  • With producer & instructional team
  • Within instructional team

Just like we emphasize with our students, feedback should be bite-sized, meaningful & regular. For instructors, this means touching base regularly to review relevant classroom data together.

  • Identifying students who need more support / resources
  • Celebrating student and class wins!
  • Student experience & program updates

Producers and instructors then agree on action steps based on the review and will follow up on these steps during the next meeting.

The format of these reviews varies by market; please talk with your producer and instructional team to set these up. See Post-ITC onboarding meeting checklist.

Data Review (Student Sentiment)

Weekly Data Review

With Instructional Team

  • What are we looking at?
  • How will we use this data?

You don’t have to wait for your producer to review student data. As an instructional team, you’ll be the first to receive many types of student data (homework assignments, projects, exit tickets), and we encourage you to utilize the feedback avenues provided to reflect on your own teaching practice and students’ program experience to make adjustments in real time.

One of the benefits of teaching at GA is teaching in team; setting up weekly debriefs with team members, within or without the producer, is a long-standing best practice with our top instructional teams.

Data Review (Student Sentiment)

Weekly Data Review

With Instructional Team

  • What are we looking at?
  • How will we use this data?

Team members often set aside time before, after, or even outside of class to review the previous week’s data, including exit tickets and student performance on homework; identify materials and support questions they have for each other and GA; and sync on the upcoming content and events of the class.

Instructional teams also find it helpful to complete class activities together that benefit from input and feedback - things like workshopping and grading student projects or planning for special class sessions. It’s often easier to tackle these large-class initiatives with your fellow instructors - and it makes the grading and feedback more consistent, too!

These are quite in-depth! These 50-question surveys, completed by students during 20 minutes of class, gauge everything from the performance of instructional team and course materials to the level of support and experience provided by GA.

Observation & Feedback (Instructional Quality)

What are we looking at?

A rubric is set of guidelines used to promote the consistent application of learning expectations and make these expectations clear to both parties (instructor and student). Rubrics help ensure consistency in evaluating work from person to person, course to course, and market to market. Rubrics are also used as evaluation tools to determine the degree to which learning goals have been demonstrated. (Adapted from edglossary.org)

GA’s Instructional Quality Rubric identifies the top seven skills that define successful instruction and associates four levels of ability with those developing skillsets.

Levels of Instructional Ability

Our goal is to help you develop into a proficient instructor. The levels that we’ve outline mark key milestones on that journey.

  1. Beginner - Learning instructional best practices at GA. New teachers.
  2. Approaching - Practicing best practices most of the time.
  3. Proficient - Executing best practices routinely. Goal for all instructors!
  4. Stretch - Optimizing best practices consistently. Goal for growth.
