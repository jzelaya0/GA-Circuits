Graduation

General Assembly measures student progress through frequent homework assignments and in-depth projects. Students are graded on a pass/fail basis. Graduation consists of the following four components:

  • Attendance
  • Homework Completion
  • Project Completion
  • Letter of Completion

Graduation

  • Attendance no more than 2 absences

  • Homework Completion 80%

  • Project Completion passing final project summative assessment

  • Letter of Completion

For students to succeed, they must not have more than two absences.

At each GA campus, attendance is taken at every class meeting.*

With prior approval from GA,

  • Full-time students: permitted to miss up to 4 class meetings.
  • Part-time students: permitted to miss up to 2 class meetings.

A class meeting is defined as the instructional hours provided on one calendar day.

Students that fail to attend 4 class meetings without advanced notice may be withdrawn, but GA may allow a greater number of excused absences at its discretion.

*In addition, at GA’s Washington campus, attendance is taken by faculty fifteen minutes after class begins. Any WA student who arrives to class more than 15 minutes late will be marked tardy. Three late arrivals will constitute one absence.

Graduation

  • Attendance no more than 2 absences

  • Homework Completion 80%

  • Project Completion passing final project summative assessment

  • Letter of Completion

For students to succeed, they must complete 80% of the homework for a course.

Because we evaluate student progress on an ongoing basis, this standard needs to be maintained in real-time throughout the course. This helps us adequately support struggling students and set student expectations regarding graduation.

Graduation

  • Attendance no more than 2 absences

  • Homework Completion 80%

  • Project Completion passing final project summative assessment

  • Letter of Completion

For students to succeed, they must receive a passing “grade” on their final project.

Because the final project acts as a summative assessment, or demonstration of the “sum” of the knowledge of the course, a passing grade is necessary for graduation. Student projects are evaluated against a project rubric which lists all of the skills students should demonstrate as part of the project that they’ve learned throughout the course.

Achieving the Goals of the Course

  • Student success includes achieving graduation status as well as the goals of the course.

  • Course goals outline the value that’s delivered or experienced as a result of the course.

  • For many of our courses, the goal includes not only learning key skills and tools, but also creating production-standard portfolio projects and understanding industry trends.

  • Your course materials will have more information on your specific course goals.

Offering student support options

  • Schedule office hours - when students can actually come! Ask them. Poll them.

  • Offer asynchronous support - tell students how you’ll be able to work with them if they can’t be at the physical office hours. Will you offer virtual office hours another time or set up individual appointments?

  • Set communication norms for support - Are you an email person? How quickly can they expect a response? When will you be available (Monday and Wednesday nights; I can give quick responses) vs. unavailable? (I can’t guarantee a response the day of class; best to ask any questions the night before).

Approach to Feedback & Grading

Giving students feedback means giving feedback ...

  • in class

  • on homework, projects, and presentations

  • during direct instruction: Confirm correct answers or indicating wrong ones. Students are looking to you as the expert to indicate if they are on track.

  • during lab or workshop time: Walk around to observe students working

Tip: Engage students during workshop time. Ask them to explain what they are doing, why, what they’d do next, etc.

Approach to Feedback & Grading

Giving students feedback means giving feedback ...

  • in class

  • on homework, projects, and presentations

  • Evaluating student work is critical to student satisfaction and success because it allows students to understand what and to what extent they are or aren’t learning.

  • We don’t give students ‘grades’ at GA, but we do expect feedback for submitted work.

    • ...But don’t comment on everything! Look for certain ‘set’ of skills or issues in each assignment. This way, you are able to evaluate the class’s progress on a smaller set of benchmarkable skills.
    • This could be as simple as providing comments about (1. what went well (2. what could be improved and (3. suggestions for next steps

Approach to Feedback & Grading

Effective feedback is…

  • timely - given as soon as possible after submitted. The more time that lapses, the less relevant feedback is

  • specific - points to specific areas, lines or parts that exhibit a particular trait. i.e. Doesn’t just offer blanket feedback or praise without showing the student where the feedback applies

  • targeted - focuses on a smaller set of skills (i.e. 3 per assignment) to keep students from being overwhelmed by feedback and instructors from nit-picking

  • optimistic - recognizes that students are learning and need encouragement to continue to do so

  • kind - approaches feedback constructively and in the same manner you would say in person
