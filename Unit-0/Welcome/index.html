Adult Learning Focus

Knowing how adults learn helps us understand our students and how to create effective, high-impact learning experiences for them.

We utilize a set of six adult learning principles to guide our approach to instruction at GA. These principles structure everything we do, from lesson design, to instructor training, to building better products.

The theory, first popularized by Malcolm Knowles in the 1980s, is based on the idea that adults need to be active learners. In other words, they need to “do” in order to learn.

Learning experiences for adults need to include

  1. Opportunities for self-direction
  2. Opportunities to draw on life experiences
  3. Material relevant to the learner
  4. Problem-centered learning approaches
  5. Social elements
  6. Active Learning

We’ll dive a little deeper into what these principles mean in terms of creating effective learning experiences in the classroom in this training.

Backward Design of Our Products

All of our products go through a rigorous research, development, building, and revision process based on the principles of “backward design.”

Backward design is a method of designing educational curricula based on the idea that you must determine the end goals first and work backward to determine how to reach these goals.

This method was popularized through Jay McTighe and Grant Wiggins’ Understanding By Design (1998), and involves three key stages:

  1. Identifying desired results
  2. Determining what evidence will show the results have been achieved
  3. Designing activities that will make desired results happen

GA Course Design Process

We approach course design from a backward design and iterative design perspective.

Backward Design

This process involves product managers, instructional designers, and subject matter experts. The premise is that one must determine the end goals of an experience first and then “working backward” to build out each incremental step.

Backward Design

  • conducting industry research
  • determining standards & learning goals
  • planning assessments that demonstrate if course goals are met
  • planning lessons & learning activities to teach students key skills

“Working backward” from the end ensures that we thoughtfully plan how to reach course goals.

GA Course Design Process

This process ensures that

  • the courses we create tech skills demanded by the industry
  • students and instructors know what success looks like at the end of the course
  • there’s a clear skills road map to achieving the course outcome
  • the course outcome is validated long before product development starts

Emphasizing Grit and Growth Mindset

In addition to teaching hard skills, our programs encourage a “GA Mindset” or approach to learning. This means that, in addition to teaching students how to approach problems, we also help them confront new challenges and work through solutions with “grit” and a “growth mindset.”

Emphasizing this kind of approach highlights GA’s emphasis on affective learning - the learning domain most concerned with the comfort or discomfort that one feels in a learning environment.

In our ever-changing environment, we’ve found that the most successful learners also embrace an approach to learning that enables them to succeed despite the discomfort caused by being challenged and learning something new.

Growth Mindset and Grit

Grit & Growth Mindset are two affective learning competencies that General Assembly teaches not only its students, but also its instructors and staff.

We teach these two key competencies to help students confront challenges and remain resilient when they fail.

Growth Mindset and Grit

GRIT

Grit, alongside self-control, is one of two key predictors of success, according to Angela Lee Duckworth. We’ve adopted her definition of grit: Grit is the tendency to sustain interest in and effort toward very long-term goals (Duckworth et al., 2007). Source.

GROWTH MINDSET

  • Someone with a growth mindset believes that intelligence is NOT fixed; it is malleable and changeable, according to an individual’s motivation to pursue continuous growth.
  • Carol Dweck’s theory of ‘mindset’ (fixed vs. growth) derives from the idea that success is based on hard work, learning, training, failing, and doggedness. People with these traits are said to have a "growth" or an "incremental" theory of intelligence (growth mindset). Source. TED talk.

What this looks like in the classroom

  • As a skill

  • For instructors

  • As a feedback mentality

  • Instructors integrate growth mindset and grit into their lessons, as classes emphasize trial and error as part of the industry norm. For example, FEWD, BEWD, iOS, AN, and DS build students’ resilience through this transparency & modeling proactive responses to failure.

What this looks like in the classroom

  • As a skill

  • For instructors

  • As a feedback mentality

  • Instructors practice growth mindset as a key competency on the instructor rubric. Can instructors adapt quickly and positively? Can they incorporate feedback from a variety of sources to make decisions that benefit the students? Do instructors demonstrate collaboration and a mindset toward improvement?

What this looks like in the classroom

  • As a skill

  • For instructors

  • As a feedback mentality

  • Giving feedback is another way growth mindset and grit can be seen in the classroom. Instructors pose difficult skills as ‘challenges’ that enhance students’ ability to succeed next time, and instructors frame challenges with the big picture goal - i.e. becoming a web developer and changing careers - to encourage grit for the 10 to 12 week intensive learning process.
